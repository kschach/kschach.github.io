Research Focus

  • The Problem: Low-income Manitobans face barriers to accessing vital income support benefits (benefit non-take-up)

  • The Program: Nonprofit and public sector agencies promote access to income benefits (benefit intermediary programs)

  • The focus of my research is the problem of benefit non-take-up and the remedy of benefit intermediary programs in Manitoba.
  • Government income benefits are vital to enabling low-income Manitobans to meet their basic needs.
  • Government transfers accounted for over half of the total incomes of Manitobans in the bottom income decile (2015).
  • However, many vulnerable community members face barriers to claiming the benefits to which theyre entitled.
  • In response to this problem nonprofit and public sectors agencies offer benefit intermediary programs that promote access to income benefits.
  • My goal for this research is to contribute to the sociological understanding of benefit intermediary programs and to produce knowledge that can inform program development.

Research Questions

  1. How do benefit intermediary programs promote access to government income benefits in Manitoba?

  2. What factors contribute to successful program outcomes?

  3. What constraints do these programs face?

Social justice

  • To answer these questions, I draw upon Nancy Frasers and Jeff Noonans social justice theory
    • Justice entails having the material resources and social respect to be participate as a peer in society participatory parity (Fraser)
    • Equitable satisfaction of material and social life-requirements (Noonan)
    • Remedies for injustice include redistribution of resources and recognition of the social status of subordinated groups.
    • Actors can employ different strategies to achieve these remedies.
    • A strategy of affirmation seeks to redress unjust outcomes in the present.
    • A strategy of nonreformist reform works for affirmative remedies and achievable reforms in the present that open up further opportunities to change the structures that produce unjust outcomes in the first place.

Social welfare

  • I also draw upon theories of social welfare (Olsen, Rice and Prince).
  • Olsen categorizes three forms of welfare provision
    • Income benefits
    • In-kind goods and services
    • Social protection legislation
  • Different institutions provide for the welfare of the population, including:
    • The state
    • The family
    • The private sector
    • The nonprofit / voluntary sector
  • The extent of state involvement varies temporally and geographically
  • In theis thesis, I focus on the income benefits that the welfare state provides, and the services that nonprofit and public agencies offer to assist community members to access these benefits.

Realist Evaluation

  • I employ a realist evaluation methodology, as put forward by Pawson and Tilley.
  • Realist evaluation asks why?
    • what works for whom in what circumstances and why (Pawson and Manzano-Santaella)
  • Context in which a program is embedded and intervenes
  • Mechanisms that produce and sustain the social problem a program seeks to solve
  • Mechanisms a program triggers to address this problem
  • Outcomes a program realizes
  • As a variant of critical realism, realist evaluation recognizes the engaged position of the researcher.
  • This is key for my study given my insider position, having worked as a practitioner in the field for ten years.
  • I drew upon my position as an insider within the field throughout this study

Context

Benefit non-take-up

  • Complicated systems and processes
  • Inadequate public and private sector assistance
  • Social factors

Benefit intermediaries

  • Assist community members to take up income benefits
  • Work to increase benefit accessibility
  • Rely upon government funding
  • These programs operate within the social liberal welfare state of Manitoba, characterized by some universal services and residual income benefits.
  • In this context, there are significant barriers to benefit take-up, including:
    • complicated benefit systems and claiming processes
    • inadequate support from the public and private sectors (inaccessible / too expensive / inappropriate )
    • social factors related to the treatment of people living in poverty, such as distrust of government institutions, and fear that benefit claims will have negative consequences.
  • To address this problem, benefit intermediary programs
    • assist community members to take up income benefits (affirmation)
    • pursue systemic changes to increase benefit accessibility (nonreformist reform)
  • Benefit intermediaries rely upon government funding to provide these programs

Affirmation: Goals

Redistribution

  • Increase service users incomes
  • Enhance service users quality of life

Recognition

  • Treat service users as peers
  • Empower service users to claim their entitlements
  • Benefit intermediaries employ a strategy of affirmation to pursue two major goals
    • Primary goal is affirmative redistribution that increases service users incomes by promoting access to income benefits that can enhance service users quality of life.
    • Secondary goal of affirmative recognition entails treating service users as respected peers throughout their program involvement.
    • Service providers also seek to empower service users to claim their entitlements and interact with the welfare state from a position of higher status.
    • This involves working together with service users to build knowledge, skills, and confidence.

Affirmation: Activities

  1. Financial literacy education & public legal education

  2. Benefit screening

  3. Tax return preparation & benefit applications

  4. Financial problem-solving, coaching, & counselling

  5. Advocacy and representation

  • Benefit intermediaries undertake a number of activities to achieve these goals.
    • Education about benefits, rights, and responsibilities;
    • Assistance to identify and apply for benefits;
    • Problem-solving and coaching when issues arise in claiming benefits;
    • Advocacy to benefit administrators;
    • Formal representation at administrative tribunals at which appeals can be made and benefit decisions are adjudicated.

Affirmation: Outcomes

Redistribution

Approx. 14,000 service users accessed $38 million of government income benefits in 2018

Recognition

Service users

  • feel heard and valued
  • gain knowledge and awareness
  • reduce stress
  • have a sense of hope
  • The redistributive outcomes of this work are clear in program reports.
    • A handful of organizations
  • Key informants also observed outcomes of recognition.
    • Service users feel heard and valued by service providers
    • Service users build knowledge, skills, and confidence that may equip them to claim their benefits
    • Service users report reduced stress and greater hope for the future.

Affirmation: Mechanisms

  • Alignment between program offerings and community need

  • Affirmative recognition for redistribution

  • I posit two key mechanisms that enable benefit intermediaries to achieve these outcomes

  • Strong alignment between program offerings and community need

    • Government income benefits are vital to meeting basic needs
    • But benefit systems are complicated and difficult to access
    • Few options for benefits assistance
    • Benefit intermediaries offer free assistance
    • They are well-located socially (connections to low-income communities) and geographically (in economically disadvantaged neighbourhoods)
    • They have developed positive reputations among low-income community members (trust, non-judgement, respect)
    • These factors account for the high use of benefit intermediary programs by low-income Manitobans

  • Succeed in redistributing income because they employ affirmative recognition in their approach to service delivery

    • Service users set own goals and determine the level of assistance they would like from service providers Taking the lead from the participant
    • Service providers respect and value service users autonomy, knowledge, and life experiences, and employ supportive counseling, coaching, and active listening skills during service delivery.

Nonreformist Reform: Goals

  • Eliminate structural barriers that inhibit benefit take-up
  • Build cross-sectoral capacity to assist low-income community members to access their benefits

Short-term

Enhance service users quality of life

Medium-term

Increase benefit accessibility

Long-term

Change welfare state systems

  • Benefit intermediaries also pursue nonreformist reforms to promote access to income benefits.
  • This work seeks to accomplish short-term, medium-term, and long-term goals.
  • Short-term: Enhance service users quality of life by increasing income and capacity to meet basic needs.
  • Medium-term: Expand the availability of free benefit assistance services to support more community members.
  • Long-term: Change welfare state systems to eliminate structural barriers so that benefits are more accessible to all community members, systems are more responsive to community members needs, and treat benefit recipients respectfully as peers.

Nonreformist Reform: Activities

  1. Direct service delivery

  2. Cross-sectoral collaboration

  3. Public policy advocacy

  4. Complaints

  • Benefit intermediaries pursue nonreformist reforms through a variety of activities.
  • Service providers learn from service users through direct service delivery, and nudge public servants to work for systems changes from within.
  • They collaborate with one another and with other sectors to expand the availability of benefit assistance services and identify solutions for systemic problems.
  • They engage with politicians and policymakers to pursue policy change and increased public investment to make benefits more accessible
  • Sometimes employ a politics of complaint to seek remedies through ombudsperson services and litigation

Nonreformist Reform: Outcomes

  • Changes to benefits legislation, regulation, policy, and practice

  • Increased investment in public sector and nonprofit benefit assistance services

  • Key informants observed that changes they have worked towards have been implemented, including:
    • Amendments to EIA regulations and practices, EI eligibility criteria, and revised CRA processes that increase benefit accessibility.
    • Welfare state agencies (such as EIA and CRA) providing more assistance to community members including the restoration of some services that had been retrenched.
    • Federal and provincial governments have increased funding for nonprofit benefit intermediary programs.
    • A growing field of practice as additional agencies offer benefit intermediary programs, or expand existing programs.
  • I find that these outcomes constitute nonreformist reforms in that they:
    • Facilitate greater access to income benefits by low-income community members, who can better meet their basic needs, enhance their quality of life, and have more freedom to pursue future goals.
    • Expand the set of options available to benefit intermediaries and other actors working for economic justice, who can use successful policy and practice changes, the broader availability of benefit intermediary services, and new sources of government funding to pursue further reforms.

Nonreformist Reform: Mechanisms

  • Learning from and amplifying service users voices

  • Partnerships

  • Flexible and strategic framing

  • I posit several mechanisms that account for the success of benefit intermediaries nonreformist reform efforts.

  • A key mechanism is combining direct service delivery with systemic work.

    • Benefit intermediaries learn from service users about their experiences, identify systemic issues, and can amplify service users voices when working for policy and practice change.
    • This epistemic privilege can enable benefit intermediaries to target the specific issues faced by the communities they serve and to provide insight to public servants who are less connected to low-income communities.

  • Partnerships are also critical for nonreformist reform.

    • Cross-sectoral partnerships enable benefit intermediaries to expand the provision of services across Manitoba.
    • As well, partnerships between agencies enable benefit intermediary programs to put forward public policy positions that are endorsed by multiple organizations, that show strength in numbers, and that insulate organizations against the potential consequences of public policy work that affects government funders.

  • Finally, I posit that benefit intermediaries use of flexible and strategic framing is key to their successful engagement with politicians, policymakers, funding agencies, and other stakeholders.

    • Benefit intermediaries may position programs in terms of social justice (i.e., as fair and equal access to justice and health equity)
    • Or strategically use economic arguments (i.e., the cost savings of harm reduction, and the return on investment of bringing federal benefits into Manitoba)

Constraints

  • Dependence upon the state

  • Operating in parallel to mainstream channels for benefit take-up

  • Limited resources

  • However, I recognize that these programs face structural constraints that limit their impact.

  • Heavily dependent on the state

    • to provide income benefits in the first place
    • to provide funding for benefit intermediary programs (direct contracts and foregone tax revenue of charitable donations)

  • Operate in parallel to mainstream channels for benefit take-up. This model

    • May stigmatize low-income community members who use alternative services
    • May contribute to system statis by reducing the incentive for welfare state agencies to change policy and practice to better meet low-income community members needs if they can channel low-income community members through benefit intermediaries

  • Resource constraints

    • Cannot fully meet community need for benefit assistance
    • Need to turn community members away when capacity is full
    • Need to focus on particular benefits, areas, or times of year

  • These conditions limit

    • the scope of direct service delivery
    • the extent to which they can pursue structural change
      • because of fear of funding loss
      • because they have to redirect scarce resources capacity away from directly meeting community needs

Conclusion

Benefit intermediary programs

  • increase service users incomes (redistribution)

  • empower service users to claim their entitlements (recognition)

  • contribute to systemic changes that increase benefit accessibility (nonreformist reform)

  • are the life-valuable alternative

  • In conclusion, I find that despite the political and economic constraints they face:

    • benefit intermediary programs increase service users income through affirmative redistribution;
    • they empower service users to claim their entitlements through affirmative recognition;
    • these two affirmative remedies enhance service users quality of life by increasing their capacity to meet their life-requirements, and providing greater opportunities to achieve social and self-esteem.
    • I also find that benefit intermediary programs contribute to systemic changes that increase the accessibility of welfare state benefits, through a strategy of nonreformist reform.

  • Jeff Noonan asserts that ethical political choices are made between the actual alternatives that exist in a social context.

  • Applying Noonans logic, I find that benefit intermediary programs are the life-valuable alternative.

    • In a context of benefit inaccessibility, benefit intermediaries assist low-income community members to take up their benefits while opening up opportunities to change the social liberal welfare state that provides these benefits.

  • These programs play a vital role in increasing the take-up of money for the marginalized.
